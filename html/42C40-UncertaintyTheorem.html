uncertainty theorem


The uncertainty principle, as first formulated by Heisenberg, states that the product of the standard deviations of two conjugated variables, cannot be less than some minimum. This statement has been generalized to a precise mathematical theorem, in the frame of wavelet theory.

1 THE UNCERTAINTY THEOREM

Let f⁢(t) be a real function of the real variable, satisfying the L2 condition (see below), and F⁢(ω) its Fourier transform. The standard deviation Δt and Δω of t and ω respectively, satisfy the following inequality:

Δt⁢Δω≥12

For this formula to make sense, Δt and Δω must be precisely defined.

2 THE 𝑳𝟐 CONDITION

A real function f⁢(t) of the real variable t will be said to satisfy the L2 condition if f⁢(t), t⁢f⁢(t) and the derivative f′⁢(t) are all in L2.
If F⁢(ω) is its Fourier transform, -i⁢ω⁢F⁢(ω) is the transform of f′⁢(t). All the following functions belong to L2:

f⁢(t),f′⁢(t),t⁢f⁢(t),F⁢(ω),ω⁢F⁢(ω)

The first three functions are just the definition and the two last ones result from Parseval’s identity, recalled here in its integral form:

∫-∞∞U⁢(ω)¯⁢V⁢(ω)⁢𝑑ω=2⁢π⁢∫-∞∞u⁢(t)¯⁢v⁢(t)⁢𝑑t

U⁢(ω) and V⁢(ω) are the Fourier transforms of u⁢(t) and v⁢(t).

3 DEFINITIONS

f⁢(t) and F⁢(ω) being in L2, we may define their finite norms:

∣∣f∣∣2=∫-∞∞∣f⁢(t)∣2⁢𝑑t   ∣∣F∣∣2=∫-∞∞∣F⁢(ω)∣2⁢𝑑ω

By Parseval’s identity, they are related:

∣∣f∣∣2=12⁢π⁢∣∣F∣∣2

We are now able to define the probability distributions T⁢(t) and Ω⁢(ω) for the ”random” variables t and ω:

T⁢(t)=∣f⁢(t)∣2∣∣f∣∣2   Ω⁢(ω)=∣F⁢(ω)∣2∣∣F∣∣2

Since the L2 integrals of T and Ω are 1, they are proper probability distributions. The mean value t0 of t is defined the usual way:

t0=∫-∞∞t⁢T⁢(t)⁢𝑑t

Note that ω’s mean value is always 0 because f⁢(t) is a real function. Finally, we have the standard deviations for the uncertainty theorem:

Δt2=∫-∞∞T⁢(t)⁢(t-t0)2⁢𝑑t   Δω2=∫-∞∞Ω⁢(ω)⁢(t-t0)2⁢𝑑ω

4 PROOF OF THE THEOREM

The heart of the proof is the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality in the L2 Hilbert space: the product of the norms of two functions u⁢(t) and v⁢(t) is greater than, or equal to, the norm of their scalar product:

∫-∞∞∣u⁢(t)∣2⁢𝑑t⁢∫-∞∞∣v⁢(t)∣2⁢𝑑t≥∣∫-∞∞u⁢(t)¯⁢v⁢(t)⁢𝑑t∣2

Equality occurs if, and only if, one of the functions is proportional to the other. For the two functions u⁢(t)=(t-t0)⁢f⁢(t) and v⁢(t)=f′⁢(t), we have therefore:

∫-∞∞(t-t0)2⁢f⁢(t)2⁢𝑑t⁢∫-∞∞f′⁢(t)2⁢𝑑t≥∣∫-∞∞(t-t0)⁢f⁢(t)⁢f′⁢(t)⁢𝑑t∣2

The integral at the right hand side can be integrated by parts. Using the definition of ∣∣f∣∣:

∫-∞∞(t-t0)2⁢f⁢(t)2⁢𝑑t⁢∫-∞∞f′⁢(t)2⁢𝑑t≥14⁢∣∣f∣∣4

But ∣∣f∣∣ and ∣∣F∣∣ are related by a 2⁢π factor, so:

∫-∞∞(t-t0)2⁢f⁢(t)2⁢𝑑t⁢∫-∞∞f′⁢(t)2⁢𝑑t≥18⁢π⁢∣∣f∣∣2⁢∣∣F∣∣2

Applying Parseval’s identity to the second integral of the left hand side, we get:

12⁢π⁢∫-∞∞(t-t0)2⁢f⁢(t)2⁢𝑑t⁢∫-∞∞ω2⁢F⁢(ω)2⁢𝑑ω≥18⁢π⁢∣∣f∣∣2⁢∣∣F∣∣2

We have used the fact that the Fourier transform of f′⁢(t) if -i⁢ω⁢F⁢(ω).
Now, dividing both sides by the norms, and simplifying by the 2⁢π factor, we get exactly the uncertainty theorem:

∫-∞∞(t-t0)2⁢T⁢(t)⁢𝑑t⁢∫-∞∞ω2⁢F⁢(ω)2⁢𝑑ω≥14

5 THE GAUSSIAN FUNCTION

The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality becomes an equality if, and only if, one of the functions is proportional to the other. In our case, this condition is expressed by f′⁢(t)=λ⁢(t-t0)⁢f⁢(t) where λ is a constant. This differential equation is readily solved: f⁢(t)=k⁢eλ⁢(t-t0)2. f⁢(t) must be in L2 so that λ must be negative. Defining λ=-12⁢σ2, we get the gaussian function in its traditional form:

f⁢(t)=e-(t-t0)22⁢σ2

The constant k has been omitted because it cancels anyway in the probability distributions. The standard deviations are easily computed from their definitions:

Δt=σ2    Δω=1σ⁢2

Their product is 12, independent of σ. There is no other function with this property.
